Case Study: A Medication Reminder App

Brief Introduction

This project, this idea, was something I could relate to because I occasionally have to take medication and, frankly, I forget about them. I have to tell you, I can’t remember things, and I just need something like this, a reminder application that is simple to use and appealing to the eye to help me remember these medications. And it’s not only me that needs it; my grandmother does as well. Because she can’t use a smartphone or an application right now, my sister or I would utilize the parental option to take care of her reminders.

Overview

The research and decisions I made when designing this medication reminder app are documented in this UI/UX case study. The app provides features such as prescription and appointment reminders, as well as a parental control function. It took 4 weeks to finish this design session.

Problem Statement

Some people find it difficult to learn new apps in this ever-expanding digital environment, and people nowadays tend to forget things more easily, such as taking their prescriptions. People need a way to remember to take their prescriptions without having to learn how to use sophisticated programs.

Main Goal

Create a basic, easy-to-use app so that users don’t forget their medicine schedules, can easily discover pharmacies and clinics near them, and can be directed through the app by their loved ones if necessary.

Information Architecture

Information architecture is a visual representation for the user all the features that may be explored within the platform and how to access any useful information quickly and easily.

User Flow

I started thinking about how the user would use the product after developing my personas. Working from their point of view, I designed a user flow to show how the user might interact with the product.

Wireframes

I created low-fidelity wireframes after analyzing IA and user flows. These are the foundations of an application's user interface that effectively organize all of the work that has been done onto a screen.

Final Design

Hi-Fidelity prototypes incorporate actual content, typefaces, color, effects, and branding aspects, as opposed to placeholder in low-fidelity wireframe. Polishing the design and making it appealing.

Splash Screen: It captures the attention of users with eye-catching graphics.
Onboarding: It is virtual unboxing exeperince that helps users get started with an app.
Sign in/ Sign up: We added a quick sign up/sign in option to make the user more comfortable.
Features: Set medication reminder, set appointment, parental control, nearby.

Conclusion

I learned from this case study not to allow my own biases and preconceptions to influence my design approach. As a designer, it is our responsibility to ensure that all design decisions to benefit our users, while simultaneously keeping the business model in mind to ensure product success.
